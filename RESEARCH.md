# Coy Family — Historical Research Notes

A working reference for storytelling, future map features, and genealogical investigation. Organized chronologically by era.

---

## 1. Origins: Lincolnshire, England (1590–1638)

**Richard Coy (c.1590–1638)** is the patriarch of the American Coy line, born and buried in Lincolnshire, England. He never emigrated. His will was dated December 2, 1637 and proved at Stamford, Lincolnshire on April 10, 1638 — the death of their father appears to have been the direct trigger for his sons Matthew and Richard emigrating to New England later that year.

Lincolnshire in the 1630s was a hotbed of Puritan nonconformity. Boston, Lincolnshire (the town the Coy brothers came from) was the very town that gave Boston, Massachusetts its name — early emigrant ships frequently recruited from the area.

---

## 2. The Whittingham Group Migration (1638)

**The key figure:** John Whittingham (baptized September 29, 1616, Sutterton/Boston, Lincolnshire; died early 1649, Ipswich, MA) was a Lincolnshire gentleman who organized a group migration of young men from the Boston, Lincolnshire area to Ipswich, Massachusetts. He recruited them as indentured servants — ten years of service in exchange for passage and living expenses.

**The group he brought in 1638:**
- Matthew Coy (born c.1622–23, Lincolnshire)
- Richard Coy (born c.1625, Lincolnshire)
- Mary Coy (their sister)
- Haniell (Hanniel) Bosworth (born c.1614–15, Boston, Lincolnshire)
- Robert Smith (born c.1626, Boston, Lincolnshire area)
- John Anniball (Annable)
- Possibly others

Matthew and Richard traveled from Boston, Lincolnshire to London, to Whittingham's home, and departed around June 24, 1638, arriving at Boston, Massachusetts in late August or early September 1638. All settled at Ipswich, Massachusetts Bay.

**The ship is unknown.** Robert Charles Anderson's *Great Migration Newsletter* Vol. 21 (2012) — the definitive scholarly source — places the departure and arrival dates but states their migration "has not yet been associated with any particular ship." Every major 1638 Great Migration ship with a surviving passenger list has been checked; none contain the Whittingham party. The most likely explanation is that no manifest survives for their vessel. The 1645 Essex County court records documenting a servant dispute may contain the ship name — this is worth investigating at Massachusetts Archives or American Ancestors/NEHGS.

**John Whittingham's background:** His mother Elizabeth (Bulkeley) Whittingham had already emigrated to Boston, MA in 1633 on the **Griffin**, traveling with her second husband Atherton Hough. Her Bulkeley family connections were among the most prominent in early Puritan New England.

**The Hubbard family — the spine of the whole network:** John Whittingham's wife was **Martha Hubbard** (c.1613–1687), born in Clacton-on-Sea, Essex, England — daughter of **William Hubbard Sr.** (1585–1670) and Judith Knapp, who settled in Ipswich, MA in 1635. Her brother was **Rev. William Hubbard Jr.** (c.1621–1704), the prominent Puritan minister of Ipswich and New England historian.

A Barton family genealogy (one-barton-family.us, b3236.html) provides further precision: Richard Coy (Matthew's brother) served **William Hubbard Sr.** directly under a 10-year indenture — the patriarch himself, not his minister son. This means Richard Coy was a servant in the household of his employer's (Whittingham's) father-in-law. Meanwhile, **Matthew Coy** served **Atherton Hough** — John Whittingham's stepfather (Hough had married Elizabeth Bulkeley Whittingham, John's mother, in 1630). Both Coy brothers thus ended up in households one step from Whittingham: Richard with his father-in-law, Matthew with his stepfather. The patriarch William Hubbard Sr. is the confirmed anchor connecting the Coy brothers' immigration (through his daughter Martha) and Richard Coy's servitude (in his own household). Note: Elizabeth Hubbard the Salem accuser traces through a *different* Hubbard family (James Hubbard / Naomi Cooke of Charlestown) — see Section 5 for the corrected analysis.

## 3. Ships Connected to the Family

### The Griffin (1633)
- Carried Elizabeth Bulkeley Whittingham (John Whittingham's mother) to Boston, 1633
- Her arrival set up the Whittingham network that later brought the Coy brothers over
- Part of the early wave of Puritan Great Migration ships

### The Planter (1635)
- 118 passengers; departed London in stages March–April 1635; arrived Boston June 7, 1635
- Master: Nicholas Trerice
- **Carried the Haffield family:** Richard Haffield (54, currier), wife Martha (42), and five daughters — including **8-year-old Martha Haffield** and **6-year-old Rachel Haffield**
- Martha Haffield grew up and married **Richard Coy** (Matthew Coy I's brother) c.1651 in Ipswich, MA — directly connecting the Planter to the Coy family
- Rachel Haffield later became **Rachel Clinton**, arrested May 28, 1692 in the Salem Witch Trials and imprisoned in Ipswich Gaol; she died destitute in 1694
- Other notable passengers: Francis Peabody (ancestor of the Peabody family of Essex County), William Wilcockson (helped found Stratford, CT in 1639)

### Unnamed vessel (1638)
- Carried Matthew Coy, Richard Coy, Mary Coy, and the Whittingham group
- Departed London c. June 24, 1638; arrived Ipswich, MA c. late August/September 1638
- Ship name not preserved in surviving records

## 4. King Philip's War & The Quaboag Raid (1675)

King Philip's War (1675–1676) was the deadliest conflict per capita in American history. The Wampanoag leader Metacom (King Philip) led a broad coalition against English settlements across New England.

**The Quaboag raid (August 2–4, 1675)** was one of the war's opening attacks. Quaboag Plantation (present-day Brookfield, MA) was ambushed by Nipmuc warriors. **Richard Coy (II)** — son of Matthew Coy I's brother Richard Coy — and his son **John Coy** were both killed in the attack on August 2, 1675, while serving in the colonial militia. The settlement was burned and abandoned.

The raid survivors fled east, and the war scattered many New England families southward and westward — directly contributing to the Coy family's gradual movement toward Connecticut, then Maryland and Virginia in later generations.

Source: [Meet the Planters: Coy — Westbrook Field / Quaboag Plantation](https://westbrookfield.org/historical-sites/quaboag-plantation/qp-coy-richard-3/)

## 5. The Coy Family & Witchcraft in New England (1656–1693)

The Coy family intersects the witchcraft hysteria at three distinct points spanning 37 years — more than any other thread in this genealogy.

### Ann Hibbins (1656) — The Property Transaction

**Ann Hibbins** (c.1605 – June 19, 1656) was the third person executed for witchcraft in Boston and one of the most prominent victims in colonial New England. She was the widow of William Hibbins, a wealthy Boston merchant and colony official, and the sister-in-law of Governor Richard Bellingham. In 1640 she was excommunicated from Boston's First Church after a prolonged dispute with carpenters she had hired, and her reputation for outspoken behavior made her a target. She was convicted by the General Court in May 1656 and hanged June 19, 1656 on Boston Common.

**The Coy connection:** In 1656 — the year of Hibbins's execution — **Matthew Coy I** purchased from Mrs. Ann (Elizabeth) Hibbins property at the corner of what is now Spring Lane and Devonshire Street in Boston. This was prime central Boston real estate, and the transaction appears to have occurred in the period immediately surrounding her trial. Matthew held this property until 1668, when — after the birth of son Samuel — he moved the family to Wenham, Essex County. This makes Matthew Coy I a direct, documented participant in the legal and property proceedings surrounding Boston's most famous pre-Salem witch execution.

### Elizabeth Hubbard (1692) — The Accuser: A Broken Connection

**Elizabeth Hubbard** (b.c.1674/75) was the oldest and most legally consequential of the Salem accusers — 17 years old when the trials began, she filed 40 legal complaints and gave testimony that led to 17 arrests, 13 hangings, and 2 deaths in prison. She was an orphan whose parents are **not identified in any surviving historical record**. She lived as a maidservant in the household of her great-aunt Rachel Hubbard Griggs and great-uncle Dr. William Griggs of Salem Village.

**A mistaken connection:** It was initially hypothesized here that Elizabeth Hubbard might be from the same Ipswich Hubbard family (William Hubbard Sr.'s line) that the Coys were connected to through John Whittingham's wife Martha and the minister William Hubbard Jr. That hypothesis does not hold. Rachel Hubbard Griggs's parents were **James Hubbard and Naomi Cooke** of Charlestown — an entirely separate Hubbard family from the Ipswich Hubbards. The two families shared the Hubbard surname in Essex County but are not documented as related. Elizabeth Hubbard traces through the James Hubbard / Naomi Cooke line, not through William Hubbard Sr.

**What is and isn't confirmed about Elizabeth Hubbard's parentage:**
- She was the granddaughter of one of Rachel's older brothers — either **Richard or Benjamin Hubbard** (children of James Hubbard)
- Her own parents' names are nowhere in the historical record — she simply appears as an orphan in the Griggs household
- Some sources list her as "Elizabeth Hobert," suggesting her mother may have been from the Hobart/Hobert family, meaning she may only have been Hubbard on her father's side
- After the trials, she possibly married a John Bennett and moved to Gloucester, but this is based on an uncertain marriage record of an "Elizabeth Hibbert"

**The Hubbard connection that does hold** is the one linking the Coy family to *Rev. William Hubbard Jr.* of Ipswich (the minister Richard Coy served) and *Martha Hubbard Whittingham* (the woman through whose marriage the Coy brothers came to New England). That is a confirmed and documented connection — just separate from Elizabeth Hubbard the accuser.

### Rachel Clinton (1692) — The Accused

**Rachel Haffield Clinton** — younger sister of Martha Haffield (Richard Coy II's wife, a Planter passenger in 1635) — was examined April 22, 1692 at Spark's Tavern in Ipswich, arrested May 28, 1692, and imprisoned in Ipswich Gaol. Rev. Hubbard of First Church and Rev. John Wise of Chebacco Parish made formal appeals for the accused. She was released January 3, 1693 and died destitute in early 1695.

Her case is an **Ipswich case**, distinct from the core Salem Village outbreak — she was a target of long-standing local grievances, not spectral evidence hysteria. The phrase "Salem Witch Trials" applies as a period label, but she was never in Salem Village. She married Lawrence Clinton in 1665.

**Naming note:** Rachel's mother was also named Martha Haffield (wife of Richard Haffield Sr., ~42 on The Planter in 1635). Sources naming Rachel's parents as "Richard and Martha Haffield" are referring to her mother — not to the daughter Martha Haffield who married Richard Coy II. The Planter manifest confirms both Rachel (~6 in 1635, b.~1629) and Martha the daughter (~8 in 1635, b.~1627) as sisters, daughters of Richard Haffield Sr.

Wenham, MA — where Elizabeth Roberts Coy (Matthew Coy I's wife) lived and died — sits approximately 10 miles from Salem, directly within the affected area.

## 6. Christopher Coy at the Siege of Yorktown (1781)

**Christopher Coy (1751–1839)**, generation 6 of the main Coy line, served in the American Revolution and was present at the Siege of Yorktown (September 28 – October 19, 1781) — the final major battle of the Revolutionary War. Cornwallis surrendered on October 19, 1781.

Christopher lived to age 88, dying in 1839. His military service is tagged in the map data.

## 7. The Westward Migration: Maryland → Kentucky → Illinois

The main Coy line's westward movement follows a pattern common among Scotch-Irish and English Protestant families in the late 18th century:

- **Maryland (1760s–1790s):** Christopher Coy and his generation lived in Washington County and Frederick County, MD — part of the Great Wagon Road corridor
- **Kentucky (c.1790–1820):** Multiple Coy branches moved into Madison County, KY (near Richmond/Berea), Garrard County, and Hardin County. Madison County, KY remained a hub for the extended family through the 1830s
- **Illinois (c.1820–1850):** The main line under Stephen Coy (1790–1840) settled Clay County, IL in southern Illinois. The Illinois Central Railroad (completed 1856, running directly through Clay County) later connected this area to Chicago and gave the next generation access to broader markets and migration routes

**Note on southern Illinois loyalties:** Central and southern Illinois in 1861 had strong economic and cultural ties to the South via the Ohio and Mississippi rivers. Many families in the area had relatives on both sides of the Civil War. This context likely shaped the Coy family's experience during the 1860s.

## 8. Illinois to Idaho: The Migration Story (1870s–1910s)

The movement of the Coy line from central Illinois (Mattoon, Mt. Zion, Decatur area) to Lewiston and Winchester, Idaho was enabled by a specific sequence of railroad infrastructure:

**The full route:**
1. **Illinois Central** (1856) — Cairo to Chicago through Clay/Coles/Shelby/Macon counties
2. **Chicago & North Western** (1869) — Chicago west to Council Bluffs, IA (across the river from Omaha)
3. **Union Pacific / Transcontinental** (1869) — Omaha west to Ogden, UT
4. **Oregon Short Line** (1884) — Built by Union Pacific; Granger, WY → Pocatello, ID → Boise → Huntington, OR; connected the UP main line to the OR&N at Huntington
5. **Oregon Railway & Navigation Co.** (1884) — Portland east along the Columbia River to Huntington, then a branch north from Wallula up the Snake River canyon to Lewiston
6. **Utah & Northern RR** (1881) — Ogden north through Pocatello, ID and up into Montana; first rail access into Idaho

**Pocatello, ID** was the critical hub where the Utah & Northern (north-south) and Oregon Short Line (east-west) intersected.

**Camas Prairie Railroad (1908)** — This line connected Lewiston up onto the Camas Prairie plateau, making Winchester, ID (where the later Coy generations settled) directly accessible. Before 1908, Winchester was reachable only by wagon up a steep grade. The railroad made it farmable and attractive almost overnight.

### Why Lewiston/Winchester specifically?

Several converging factors:

- **Potlatch Lumber Company** (founded 1903, near Lewiston) — harvested the vast Clearwater timber stands; actively recruited Midwestern laborers and built a company town; the largest white pine lumber operation in the world at its peak
- **Nez Perce War (1877)** — After Chief Joseph's surrender, the Camas Prairie was opened to white homesteading
- **Cheap farmland** — The Camas Prairie plateau had fertile volcanic soil ideal for wheat and peas
- **1893 Economic Panic** — Collapsed Illinois grain prices drove many farm families to seek cheaper western land
- **Big Blowup Fire (1910)** — Catastrophic firestorm burned approximately 3 million acres in Idaho and Montana over 48 hours (August 20–21, 1910). Created a massive surge in Lewiston-area logging and rebuilding employment. At least 85 firefighters were killed.

## 10. Newspaper Clippings — The Nicholas W. Coy Family, Decatur Era (c.1900–1929)

Four newspaper clippings from the Decatur, IL area document this generation of the family in detail. People identified:

### Nicholas W. Coy (Gen 9, 1842–1912) — "The Old Soldier on the Island"

Two clippings concern Nicholas W. Coy directly. One, headlined **"OLD NICHOLAS COY DIES IN QUINCY HOME,"** reports that he was "an old soldier who lived in a little shack in the river bottoms near Decatur for a long time" before dying at the Soldiers' Home in Quincy, IL. The funeral was held in Quincy and he was buried there.

A second clipping, **"THOUGHT HE SHOULD BE IN — Sheriff Did Not See Necessity of Going After Old Man Nicholas Coy,"** describes the Macon County DA alerting the Sheriff that no smoke had been seen from Coy's fire on his river island for two days in cold weather. The Sheriff declined to intervene, noting that "for the last three or four years he has been camping out" and was presumably hardened to it. This paints a vivid picture of an elderly Civil War veteran living rough on the Sangamon or Kaskaskia riverbank near Decatur — a local character known to county officials.

### Nicholas W. Coy Jr. (Gen 10, 1877–1955) — Family in Decatur

A clipping headlined **"EARL EDWIN COY"** reports the death of his 2-year-old son at the family home at **1522 North Gulick Avenue, Decatur, IL**. Earl Edwin died of diphtheria and was buried at Greenwood Cemetery with no public funeral (standard protocol for highly contagious diseases). The obituary lists five surviving brothers at home: **Carl, Robert, Roy, Ernest, and Walter Coy** — siblings of Donald R. Coy (Gen 11, main line) not previously known from other sources.

### Ernest Coy (Gen 11, d.1929) — Industrial Accident

The fourth clipping, from the **Decatur Herald, October 8, 1929**, reports: *"Man Killed, Two Hurt; Scaffold Falls 80 Feet — Workmen Wrecking Roof Forms on Shellabarger Soy Bean Mill Plunge to Bottom of Grain Bin."* The dead man is identified as **Ernest Coy**, living at West Eldorado Drive, Pines addition, Decatur. He died almost instantly. This is the same Ernest Coy listed as a brother of Donald R. Coy in the Earl Edwin obituary.

### Lineage Note

Donald R. Coy (Gen 11, main line, b.1916) is the youngest or near-youngest of at least seven children of Nicholas W. Coy Jr. and Lydia Ellen Mackey. The five brothers — Carl, Robert, Roy, Ernest, Walter — have been added to the tree as Gen 11 branches. Their direct lineage relationship to the main Idaho line is unconfirmed; they appear to have remained in Decatur while Donald migrated west.

## 12. The Coy–Marshall Connection (Maryland & Kentucky, c.1770–1812)

The Coy and Marshall families were closely connected across two states through shared military service, a joint migration to Kentucky, adjacent land grants, and intermarriage. The connection is to the **John Marshall Sr. family of Hampshire/Frederick County, MD** — a distinct line from Chief Justice John Marshall's family (Colonel Thomas Marshall of Fauquier County, VA). A hypothesis that Mark Marshall Sr. was a sibling of the Chief Justice was investigated and is **not supported by the evidence**.

### What Is Confirmed

**Shared militia unit — the two fathers.** John L. Coy I and **John Marshall Sr.** (1740–1801) both served in the **Maryland Militia, 2nd Class**. John Marshall Sr. enlisted January 13, 1777 and was discharged to invalids June 1, 1779; John L. Coy I served in the same unit during the same period. This is the root of the entire Coy-Marshall relationship.

**Joint migration to Nelson County, KY.** After the Revolution, both families migrated to Nelson County, Kentucky (Bardstown area). Mark Marshall Sr. received a **376-acre land grant in 1786** near Rolling Fork and Younger's Creek, Nelson County — the same area where John L. Coy I held adjacent land. John Marshall Sr. died in Nelson County on August 31, 1801, and is buried at Marshall Farm Cemetery, New Haven, KY.

**Rebecca Coy marries Mark Marshall, 1788.** John L. Coy I's daughter **Rebecca (Coy) Marshall** (b.1766, Washington County, MD; d.1812, Nelson County, KY) married **Mark Marshall Sr.** on **May 26–28, 1788**, in Bardstown, Nelson County, KY — converting the militia and neighborhood relationship into kinship.

**Mark Marshall Sr.'s confirmed parentage:**
- Father: **John Marshall Sr.** (b.1740, Hampshire County, VA [now WV]; d.1801, Nelson County, KY) — Maryland Militia, 2nd Class
- Mother: **Deborah Chambers Marshall** (1730–1777)
- Siblings: Robert Marshall Sr. (b.1762), Mary (Marshall) Blue (b.1764), John Marshall (b.1770), Hannah (Marshall) Chambers (b.1773)

### The Chief Justice Hypothesis — Resolved

The hypothesis that Mark Marshall Sr. was a sibling of **Chief Justice John Marshall** (1755–1835) does not hold. Chief Justice John Marshall was the son of **Colonel Thomas Marshall** (1730–1802) and **Mary "Polly" Isham Keith** of Fauquier County, VA — an entirely separate family. Paxton's *The Marshall Family* (1885), the definitive genealogy, lists all 15 of Colonel Thomas Marshall's children by name; no Mark or Marcus appears. Colonel Thomas Marshall's will (1798) names his sons explicitly: John, Thomas, James M., Charles, William, Alexander K., and Louis. The Marshall sons who moved to Kentucky settled in Fayette, Mason, and Woodford counties — not Nelson County.

The surname match and compatible birth years were coincidental. "Marshall" was one of the most common surnames in colonial Virginia and Maryland, and the Hampshire/Frederick County line (John Marshall Sr.) and the Fauquier County line (Colonel Thomas Marshall) were distinct families.

The actual militia connection — John L. Coy I and John Marshall Sr. serving together — is the genuine and documented link. Two men who had shared wartime service migrated together to Kentucky, took up neighboring land, and their children married. That is a significant and well-evidenced family bond, even without the Chief Justice.

## 17. Nicholas W. Coy — River Bottoms, War Trauma, and the Sangamon (June 2026)

### The Geography: Where the Island Was

The **Sangamon River** runs east-to-west through Decatur, and in Nicholas's lifetime it was a free-flowing river — not yet impounded. **Lake Decatur did not exist until 1920–1922**, when the city dammed the Sangamon (construction began July 1920; 30,000 barrels of concrete) to supply water for the corn refining industry. Nicholas died in 1912 — ten years before the dam. **His river island is now submerged under Lake Decatur.**

Before the dam, the Sangamon's floodplain near Decatur averaged about **half a mile wide**. The river's low gradient and high sediment load produced a winding, braided course with cut-off meanders, side channels, oxbow pools, and genuine mid-stream islands — exactly the geography described in his data.js notes ("shack on a river island in the bottoms"). These bottomlands were wooded, seasonally flooded, outside city jurisdiction, effectively ungoverned, and accessible to anyone willing to live with the floods and mosquitoes. His location was most likely in the eastern or southeastern bottoms where the river entered Decatur from Coles/Macon county direction — familiar terrain for a man who'd grown up in the same river-bottom culture of east-central Illinois.

The river bottoms near Decatur in the 1890s–1910s were also the era's equivalent of a skid row. Decatur was a significant railroad junction; railroad towns accumulated populations of transient men — discharged soldiers, day laborers, drifters — camping in river margins and rail-yard edges. Nicholas would not have been alone there.

---

### Was He Scarred from the War?

The pattern of his life strongly suggests yes, though we don't yet have his regiment or pension file to confirm specific wounds or diagnoses.

**The era's language for war trauma:** "Soldier's heart" or "irritable heart" — the Civil War equivalents of what we now call PTSD. Documented symptoms included: rapid pulse, anxiety, difficulty breathing, bad dreams, excessive sweating, dizziness, emotional numbness, sudden agitation, and inability to sustain steady work or relationships. Army physicians attributed these to physical breakdown from marching, malnutrition, and sleep deprivation rather than psychological injury — the concept of psychological trauma as a clinical entity didn't exist yet. A 2006 medical study found a **51% increase in cardiac, gastrointestinal, and nervous disease** among Civil War veterans compared to men who didn't serve. Severely affected men were simply considered "broken down" or morally weak. There was no treatment.

**Nicholas's service context:** He enlisted around 1861–62 at age 19–20. Illinois regiments in that period rotated through some of the war's most brutal engagements — Shiloh (April 1862), Vicksburg (1863), the Atlanta Campaign (1864), Sherman's March (1864–65). A full-term Illinois soldier would have experienced sustained violence, mass casualties, and epidemic disease over four years of the most formative years of his life.

---

### What Drove Him to the River — the Converging Forces

No single cause; a confluence:

**1. No safety net existed.** There was no VA, no Social Security, no mental health system, no workers' compensation. A veteran either supported himself, relied on family, scraped by on a pension ($8–12/month average in the 1880s–90s), or fell through entirely. Men who couldn't maintain employment had nowhere to go except family charity, the county poor farm — which was considered deeply shameful — or the soldiers' home.

**2. Deep early instability.** His father William died when Nicholas was **9**. His mother Dorcas remarried Samuel Thomas when he was **16** — a stranger's household, right before the war. He went to war without roots and came back to a stepfather's home rather than his own.

**3. Forty years of drift after the war.** His children's birth places trace a man who never settled:
- Shelby County (1867–70) — nowhere near family
- Hillsboro/Montgomery County (1875) — moving west
- Mt. Zion/Macon County (1877) — Decatur orbit at last
- Mattoon (1879) — back east again
- Neoga/Cumberland (1888) — still drifting, 23 years post-war

A man who can't stay in one place for more than a few years is showing the pattern.

**4. Likely separation from Elizabeth Rogers.** She died in 1921 in Taylorville (Sangamon County). He died in 1912 at the Quincy Soldiers' Home, 85 miles away. Their son Nicholas Jr. was established in Decatur. At some point — probably by the 1890s — Nicholas stopped living with his family and moved to the river alone.

**5. Known to the Macon County Sheriff's office.** The data.js note specifies he was "a local character known to the Sheriff's office" and that "local papers reported concern about him surviving winter outdoors." This is the profile of chronic, visible indigence — a man checked on repeatedly over years, possibly detained for vagrancy, before the state eventually moved him to Quincy.

**6. The river bottoms were a practical refuge.** Subsistence was possible there: fish, small game, wood for fuel, fresh water. It was outside town, invisible, free to occupy. For a man from the flatboat-and-frontier tradition of east-central Illinois, living off a river was familiar work — not degradation but retreat to something older and simpler.

---

### The Quincy Soldiers' Home: An Institutional Death

The **Illinois Soldiers' and Sailors' Home at Quincy** (est. 1885, Adams County) was 200 miles west of Decatur on the Mississippi River. Men didn't go there voluntarily as a retirement; they were sent when they had nothing left. By 1884 the eligibility rules required only that a veteran be honorably discharged and "unable to support himself due to a disability" — the disability no longer needed to be service-related. Admission records from this home (database: `ilsos.gov/isaveterans/soldSailSearch.do`) include regiment, company, rank, county of origin, occupation, marital status, and date of admission — **this record is the highest-priority document for Nicholas.** "COY" appears in the Tampico Historical Society's partial index but is not yet transcribed.

Nicholas died at the home in 1912 and was buried in Quincy. His wife was in Taylorville. His son was in Decatur. He died among strangers, in a government facility, far from the Sangamon island that no longer exists.

### Mt. Zion, Illinois — Where He Was Born

Mt. Zion sits **~8 miles south of Decatur** in Macon County, connected to Decatur by the Illinois Central Railroad branch laid out in 1860. By the time Nicholas Jr. was born in 1877 it had roughly 400 residents — a small agricultural community of corn, oats, wheat, and grazing land on productive prairie. First settlers came from Kentucky and Tennessee starting in 1827 (the same migration corridor as the Coys from the generation before), and the township had churches, mills, and schools by the Civil War. Both Nicholas Jr. (b.1877) and his wife **Lydia Ellen Mackey** (b.1882) were Mt. Zion natives — they likely grew up in the same small town before the family moved north to Decatur, settling at **1522 North Gulick Ave**.

Earnest's death on **October 8, 1929** — the same week as the stock market crash — is an exact timestamp: the family was still whole in Decatur at that moment. The Depression years that followed drove the migration west.

---

### The Idaho Migration — Why Lewiston/Winchester, Why Then

The family moved sometime between **1930 and the mid-1930s**, almost certainly Depression-driven. By 1940 when Lydia died, Winchester was home. By the 1950s, Nicholas Jr. and Donald were in Lewiston; Walter ended up across the Snake River in Clarkston, WA.

**What they were leaving — the push:**
Decatur's economy ran on corn and soybean processing (Staley, Shellabarger), railroad yards, and factory work. The 1929 crash gutted it. Earnest had just been killed in a mill accident. Illinois farm prices had been collapsing since the early 1920s as post-WWI European farms recovered. By the early 1930s, wages fell, mills cut shifts, and hundreds of Midwestern families were jumping freight trains west. The family had no specific reason to stay and every economic reason to go.

**What they were going to — the pull:**

Three concrete draws to the Lewiston-Winchester corridor:

1. **The Clearwater Mill, Lewiston** — Built in the early 1920s and later described as the **world's largest white pine mill**, the Clearwater Timber operation (which merged with Potlatch Forests in 1931) employed hundreds of workers in Lewiston. For men from Decatur's mill and rail culture, this was familiar industrial work on a new frontier.

2. **Winchester and the Camas Prairie wheat belt** — The **Camas Prairie Railroad** (built 1908–09, joint NP/UP venture) connected Lewis County's rolling wheat prairie to Lewiston and the broader rail network. The railroad was built specifically to tap "the rolling, fertile hills of the Camas Prairie and the timber of the forested hills." Winchester peaked at **745 residents in 1920** — still a growing, actively settling community when the Coys arrived. An organized wheat harvest in the 1920s Camas Prairie required 120 men and 320 horses moving farm to farm; labor demand was real.

3. **Recently opened land** — The Nez Perce Reservation had only opened to white settlement in **November 1895** — barely 35 years before the Coys arrived. Winchester itself was named in 1900. For a family with a generations-deep tradition of frontier movement (Virginia → Kentucky → Clay County IL → now Idaho), this felt familiar.

**Chain migration** — Carl (b.1900) almost certainly went first, found work, and reported back. The others followed in sequence. This is the standard pattern for Depression-era Midwest-to-Pacific-Northwest migration.

---

### The Geographic Cluster They Landed In

The family settled in a tight triangle around the **Snake River confluence**:

- **Lewiston, ID** — At 738 feet, the lowest point in Idaho; sits at the confluence of the Snake and Clearwater rivers; the furthest inland seaport in the Pacific Northwest (river navigation runs 465 miles to the Pacific via Snake → Columbia). Home of the Clearwater/Potlatch mill complex. Where Nicholas Jr. (d.1955) and Donald (d.1957) died.
- **Winchester, ID** (~30 miles southeast on the Camas Prairie) — Where Carl (d.1946) and Lydia (d.1940) died; wheat and logging town; sawmill operated until 1965; devastating fire in November 1964 effectively ended the town's commercial era.
- **Clarkston, WA** (directly across the Snake River from Lewiston) — Where Walter (d.1975) died; essentially the Washington-side twin of Lewiston; shares the same valley economy.

The Camas Prairie Railroad threaded all three nodes together. Lewiston was the hub; Winchester was the agrarian spoke; Clarkston was the Washington shore.

---

### The Migration as Continuity

Each generation of Coys moved when opportunity dried up and a frontier opened. Richard Coy left Lincolnshire for Boston (1638). Matthew moved from Boston to Wenham to Quaboag. John L. Coy I moved from Maryland to Kentucky. Stephen moved from Kentucky to Clay County, Illinois. William and Dorcas drifted east across Illinois. Nicholas Sr. drifted across the state and ended on a river. Nicholas Jr. picked up his family and crossed the Rockies.

The Lewiston valley was the last frontier the Coy line touched — the river bottoms became the Snake River, the flatboat became the rail freight car, and the Shellabarger mill became the Clearwater mill. The pattern held for nearly 300 years.

## 20. German vs. English Origin — The Jacob Coy Question (June 2026)

### The Claim

The [leebrick-liebrich genealogy tree](https://www.leebrick-liebrich.me/gp3525.htm) and corroborating Geni profiles propose a direct German line into the Christopher Coy family:

> **Jacob COY** (b. ~1700, Rheinland-Pfalz, Germany; d. ~1738 at sea) + Susana  
> → **John Coy Sr.** (b. ~1725, Germany)  
> → **Christopher Coy** (b. 1751, Somerset MD)

Jacob and Susana are claimed to have died "at sea" around 1738, presumably during or just after immigration, leaving John as an orphaned immigrant. John then settled in Maryland, took an Oath of Allegiance in 1778, and moved to Kentucky.

### The Conflict with Our Current Tree

Our data.js traces John L. Coy I (b.1725) through the **English/New England line**:

> **Matthew Coy III** (b.1694, Beverly MA; d. Stonington CT) + Sarah Welch  
> → **John L. Coy I** (b.1725, Preston CT; d.1787, Hardin KY)  
> → **Christopher Coy** (b.1751, Somerset MD)

These are mutually exclusive. Both theories claim the same historical person — the John Coy who married Sarah Woodbury on October 27, 1745, at Bristol, RI, had Christopher and at least 11 other children, and died in Kentucky in 1787.

### Primary Sources Found

**What IS documented about this John Coy (confirmed primary sources):**
1. **Marriage**: John Coy married Sarah Woodbury, October 27, 1745, Bristol, RI — family group sheet (Ramage F1333); date and place consistent across multiple sources
2. **1778 Patriot's Oath, Washington County MD**: Both "John Coy" (entry #82) and "John Coy Jr." (entry #97) appear in Samuel Barrits' Returns, March 16, 1778 — same magistrate, father-son pair confirmed in Washington County, MD
3. **Children**: Christopher, William David, Rebecca, Samuel, Daniel, Jonathan Woodbury, Huldy, Phoebe, Benjamin Benedict, Matthew Benjamin, and others — consistent across WikiTree, Geni, and leebrick-liebrich (Sarah Woodbury as source of most)
4. **Death**: ~1787, Kentucky (Nelson/Garrard County area) — coinciding with cholera outbreak; wife Sarah also died 1787

**What is NOT documented (no primary source found):**
- Jacob Coy's birth in Rheinland-Pfalz
- Jacob Coy's death "at sea ~1738"
- Any German passenger list entry for Jacob/Susana/John Coy
- Matthew Coy III having a son named John
- Any direct record naming John L. Coy I's father

### The Two-Wife Resolution

The leebrick-liebrich tree gives Huldy Coy's (b.1775) mother as **Anna DeWitt**, not Sarah Woodbury. Our data.js has both Christopher (b.1751) and Huldy listed as children of John L. Coy I. The 24-year gap between them and the different named mothers strongly suggests **two sequential marriages**:
- **(1) Sarah Woodbury**, m. 1745 → had Christopher (b.1751), William David, Rebecca, Samuel, Daniel, Jonathan Woodbury, and the older children
- Sarah Woodbury died ~1765–1773 (death date in data.js: 1787, but this may be wrong — could be conflated with the 1787 cholera deaths)
- **(2) Anna DeWitt**, m. ~1773 → had Huldy (b.1775), Phoebe, Benjamin Benedict, Matthew Benjamin, and the younger children

"DeWitt" is a Dutch/German-origin surname common in Maryland/Virginia in this era. If John (regardless of his own origin) married a woman of Dutch/German background as his second wife, it fits either origin theory.

### Evidence Weighting

**For the English/Connecticut line (current data.js):**
- Preston, CT birth is geographically consistent — Sarah Welch (Matthew III's wife) died in Preston CT; the family had roots there
- Marriage in Bristol, RI (1745) fits a New England-origin man; less natural for a German immigrant
- The Lincolnshire → Massachusetts → Connecticut migration for the Coy surname is well-documented from 1638 onward for the early generations
- Gale Ion Harris, "Early Coy and Harris Families of Eastern Connecticut," *NEHGR* Vol. 168 (2014), p.70 documents the Connecticut Coy families — though this article appears to cover a branch through Jonathan Coy, not Matthew III

**For the German line (Jacob Coy, Rheinland-Pfalz):**
- Rhineland-Palatinate immigration to Maryland/Pennsylvania 1720s–1740s was massive and well-documented; many German families settled in Frederick/Washington counties exactly where John appears
- "Coy" or "Koy" as anglicizations of German surnames (possibly "Kuh," "Keu," or a place-derived name) is plausible
- The claim that Jacob and Susana "died at sea" is conveniently unfalsifiable — but also a specific enough claim that someone may have had a source for it
- The 1778 oath does not prove German origin, but German immigrants were often specifically required to take loyalty oaths and were scrutinized more carefully during the Revolution
- "Anna DeWitt" as a second wife fits a family with German/Dutch community connections

**Conclusion: neither is proven.** The English line has better geographic/contextual fit for the marriage and early life evidence. The German line has contextual plausibility and some tree support, but no primary source yet found. The English line connection between Matthew Coy III and John L. Coy I is itself an inference without a confirmed primary source.

### The DAR Warning Is Directly Relevant

DAR record A027112 (Christopher Coy, pension W9798) carries: **"PROBLEMS HAVE BEEN DISCOVERED WITH AT LEAST ONE PREVIOUSLY VERIFIED PAPER. FUTURE APPLICANTS MUST PROVE CORRECT LINEAGE."**

This warning is almost certainly about the unresolved parentage chain Christopher → John → [Matthew III OR Jacob]. At least one DAR applicant who claimed descent through this line submitted a paper that the DAR reviewing committee found unsupportable. This is not proof that either line is wrong — it means the documentation gap is real and recognized.

## 21. John L. Coy I Parentage — RESOLVED (June 2026)

### The Answer

**John L. Coy I (b. July 18, 1725, baptized Preston CT; d. 1787, Hardin/Garrard County KY)** was the son of **Jonathan Coy (b. May 6, 1687, Norwich CT; d. February 10, 1766, Preston CT)** — not Matthew Coy III as our data.js previously had.

**Source**: Gale Ion Harris, PhD, FASG, "Early Coy and Harris Families of Eastern Connecticut," *The New England Historical and Genealogical Register*, Vol. 168, p.69 (2014). This is a peer-reviewed academic article by a Fellow of the American Society of Genealogists — the highest credential in American genealogy — published in the oldest and most authoritative American genealogical journal. It is based on primary records (baptisms, probate, town records) from Connecticut.

The German Jacob Coy theory (Rheinland-Pfalz → Jacob → John) is ruled out by this documentation. Jonathan Coy and his son John are thoroughly documented in the New England Coy line.

### What Was Wrong in the Previous Tree

### The Two Puritan Migrations — A Parallel

William Brewster sailed on the **Mayflower, 1620** as the founding Elder of Plymouth Colony. Matthew Coy I sailed from Boston, Lincolnshire in **1638** as part of the Puritan Great Migration. Both were English Separatist/Puritan men, both from the same general religious movement, 18 years apart. Their grandchildren married each other at Brewster's Neck, Connecticut, around 1684 — two Puritan migration families converging on the same plot of New England ground. The Coy line (Lincolnshire → Massachusetts → Connecticut) met the Brewster line (Nottinghamshire → Leiden → Plymouth → Connecticut) in the second generation after both families reached America.

### The Potlatch Thread

The central fact of the Lewiston Coy family is **Potlatch Corporation**. Ernest Dale (journeyman electrician) and Bill (papermaker/paper machine boss) each worked there for **exactly 38½ years** — an almost implausible coincidence that the family likely noted with pride. Their grandfather Nicholas Jr. almost certainly came to Lewiston because of the Clearwater Mill (world's largest white pine mill, early 1920s); their father Donald spent his brief adult life in that economy; and two sons built full parallel careers there. The mill was the anchor of this branch for three generations.

### The Early-Death Pattern

A striking thread across the direct male line:
- William Coy (gen 8): d.1851, age ~37
- Nicholas W. Coy Sr. (gen 9): d.1912, age ~70 — broke the pattern, but under hard circumstances
- Donald R. Coy (gen 11): d.1957, age 41
- Ernest Dale Coy Sr. (gen 12): d.2013, age 76 — the pattern finally breaks cleanly

## 28. Richard Coy of Lincolnshire — Origins Research (June 2026)

### What We Know About Richard Coy (c.1590–1638)

Richard Coy is the patriarch of the entire American Coy line. He never emigrated. His will was dated **December 2, 1637** and proved at **Stamford, Lincolnshire, April 10, 1638** — his death was almost certainly the direct trigger for sons Matthew and Richard departing for New England that same year.

**Wife:** Lucy Lenten (also recorded as "Luciana Lenton"). Her surname is itself a **Lincolnshire locational name**, derived from the village of **Lenton** in Beltisloe Hundred, approximately 12 miles north of Stamford (and ~30 miles south of Boston). Lenton's parish records extend to 1538 (Bishop's Transcripts from 1561). This creates a tight geographic cluster: Richard's will proved at Stamford + Lucy's surname pointing to a village just north of it both place the family in **south Kesteven, Lincolnshire** — a corridor between Stamford and Boston.

**Children:** Matthew (b.c.1622–23), Richard (b.c.1625), and Mary/Lucy (b.c.1623) all emigrated in 1638. The sons are identified in Whittingham group records as from "Boston" — Boston, Lincolnshire, the great Puritan port ~35 miles north of Stamford.

**What is not yet found:** No record of Richard's parents or grandparents. He does not appear in *Lincolnshire Pedigrees* (those visitation records covered only armigerous gentry; the Coys were almost certainly yeomen or tradespeople). The FamilyTreeDNA Coy project names him as the documented patriarch but records no ancestors above him.

---

### The Coy Surname — Three Origin Theories

**1. Locational from Quy, Cambridgeshire (strongest documented case)**

The place now called **Stow-cum-Quy** in Cambridgeshire was spelled "Coeia" in the 12th century and means **"Cow Island"** from Old English *cū* (cow) + *ēg* (island) — it was a hummock in the East Anglian fens. The surname emerged from people who came from or held land there:

| Record | Year | Source |
|---|---|---|
| Felice de Quoy | 1273 | Hundred Rolls of Cambridge |
| Walkelin de Queye | 1292 | Cambridgeshire Pipe Rolls |
| Ralph de Quoye | 1292 | Cambridgeshire Pipe Rolls |

All early surname records are Cambridgeshire. The geographic fit is compelling: Stow-cum-Quy is only **~25 miles from Stamford**. A family from the Quy manor area migrating north into Kesteven over a generation or two before Richard's birth c.1590 would explain everything.

**2. Old French nickname "coi" (plausible, parallel origin)**

The word *coi* (earlier *quei*) in Old French meant "quiet, calm, still" — from Latin *quietus* (the root of the English adjective "coy"). "Walter le Coy" is recorded in 12th-century England as a nickname surname. This is an entirely separate etymology from the Cambridgeshire place-name; some Coy families may trace to this rather than Quy.

**3. Breton noble "Coy" family (almost certainly unrelated)**

A noble Breton family named Coy is documented in parishes of Coray and Trégourez (Finistère, France), with records from 1440 to 1636 and a heraldic blazon of "D'or à trois trèfles d'azur" (gold with three blue trefoils). This is almost certainly a coincidental surname overlap — the Breton name would derive from Cornouaille Breton roots, not English, and has no documented connection to the Lincolnshire line.

**The DNA verdict: 6 haplogroups**

The FamilyTreeDNA Coy project (54 members, admin: Cynthia Coy) identified **6 distinct Y-DNA haplogroups** among American Coy descendants. This is significant: it means the surname was adopted independently by at least 6 unrelated male lineages, consistent with either a locational name (multiple families leaving Quy) or a common nickname (multiple men called "quiet"). The Richard Coy of Lincolnshire line is one of those 6; his specific haplogroup has not yet been tested by a known male-line descendant.

---

### St Botolph Boston, Lincolnshire — Confirmed Parish Presence

The **FreeREG unique surname index** for St Botolph Boston — the great medieval parish church of Boston, Lincolnshire, one of the largest in England — lists **COY, COYE, and COYEE** as registered surnames. This is direct confirmation that the Coy family appears in Boston's parish records. Specific baptism/marriage/burial entries have not yet been retrieved but are accessible via FreeREG or FamilySearch.

---

### The Lucy Lenten / Lenton Connection

If Lucy's surname is locational (as the evidence strongly suggests), her family came from **Lenton village, Beltisloe Hundred, Kesteven** — roughly 12 miles north of Stamford and 23 miles south of Boston. The Lenton surname is well established as a Lincolnshire locational name; a Lenton, Nottinghamshire origin is a secondary possibility but less geographically coherent given where Richard's will was proved.

A plausible working model: the Coy family held roots in **south Kesteven** (Stamford belt), Richard met Lucy from nearby Lenton, and the connection to **Boston** came through the Puritan emigration network — Boston being the hub from which John Whittingham recruited his indenture party, not necessarily the family's ancestral hometown. John Cotton's preaching at St Botolph drew Puritan families from across Lincolnshire from 1612 onward.

### Richard Coy's Will — Content Findings (Addendum, June 2026)

#### The Hunt Citation — Confirmed

**Hunt, John G. "Origin of Matthew and Richard Coy, in 1638 of Boston, Mass." *New England Historical and Genealogical Register* 116 (January 1959): 236.**

The WikiTree profile for Matthew Coy (Coy-159) cites this article directly, confirming Vol. 116, page 236. Volume 116 of NEHGR corresponds to 1959; the volume numbering runs from 1847 forward with one volume per year. Post-1927 volumes require an **American Ancestors (NEHGS) subscription** — not freely available on Archive.org or HathiTrust. It may be accessed for free at a FamilySearch Center or affiliate library.

#### Will Date — Reverted to December 2, 1637

The WikiTree profile (citing Hunt directly) gives the will date as **December 2, 1637** — not December 22. The Barton compiled genealogy's "December 22" was likely a transcription error expanding "2" to "22." Proved date confirmed: **April 10, 1638**, at Stamford.

#### What the Will Contains (and Does Not Contain)

The most significant fact about Richard Coy's will is what it **omits**. Per the probate calendar and Barton compiled genealogy:

> *"The document provided neither places nor names of wife or children."*

Richard is not named as residing anywhere specific. His wife is not named. His children are not named individually. The will states only that each child receives **£5** if the widow remarries. This is why connecting the will to the American Coy line required Hunt's scholarship — the document itself does not establish the family link.

#### The Three Witnesses

The will's witnesses are:

| Witness | Significance |
|---|---|
| **John Lenten** | The only place the Lenten surname appears in direct documentary connection with Richard Coy; almost certainly a relative of the mother |
| **Adam Townsend** | Unknown; likely a neighbor or associate |
| **John Snart** | Unknown |

John Lenten as a witness directly corroborates the identification of the mother as "Lenten" by family name, even though the will does not name her.

#### The Mother's Identity — First Name Not in Hunt

WikiTree (Coy-159) lists the mother as **"Unknown (Lenten) Coy"** — birth name Lenten, first name unknown from primary sources. The name "Lucy" or "Lucian" that appears in many compiled genealogies (Barton, MyHeritage) is a **secondary-source addition not traceable to Hunt's article**. WikiTree's entry, which is the most carefully sourced profile and cites Hunt directly, declines to assign a first name.

What is established: her birth surname was **Lenten**. Anything beyond that requires verification against the actual Hunt article or Lincolnshire parish records.

#### The Sibling Who Married John Lake — Mary, Not Lucy

A key error in the secondary literature: the Pane-Joyce genealogy (clarku.edu) records "a daughter Lucy who married Mr. John Lake." **This is wrong.** WikiTree (Coy-159, citing Hunt) records the sibling as **Mary (Coy) Lake** (WikiTree ID: Coy-161), born c. 1612, Boston, Lincolnshire. John Lake was a tailor of Boston, Massachusetts; Mary was his first wife. WikiTree Bulkeley-440's research notes flag this exact "Lucy/Mary" confusion as the source of erroneous Coy connections in the Bulkeley line.

Mary came to New England in 1638 alongside Matthew and Richard. By 1641 she was serving as a domestic to **Atherton Haugh of Boston** (the same employer as Matthew, who served as Haugh's barber).

#### The Bulkeley Claim — Rejected for the Concord Branch

WikiTree (Coy-159) explicitly states: *"There was no relationship with the Bulkeleys of Concord as has been claimed."*

The "Bulkeleys of Concord" are the family of **Rev. Peter Bulkeley** (1583–1659), who founded Concord, Massachusetts. The note specifically rejects the claim that the Coy siblings had a "nephew/uncle relationship" with this branch. The previously circulating claim (found in Barton and summarized in an earlier version of these research notes) that Hunt's methodology rested on a Bulkeley family connection should be treated as unverified pending direct access to the Hunt article. It may have originated from garbling of the Pane-Joyce "Lucy → John Lake" error or from another compiled genealogy that misread the Coy–Lake–Bulkeley social network.

**What this means:** The Barton genealogy's narrative about Hunt building the case through an "uncle/nephew relationship with Edward Bulkeley" may be accurate for a *different* Edward Bulkeley — specifically Edward Bulkeley of Chelmsford (Bulkeley-440, born Odell, Bedfordshire; died 1696 Chelmsford, MA), not the Concord family. But until the Hunt article is read directly, this distinction cannot be confirmed.

#### The 1638 Emigration Route

Per WikiTree (Coy-159):
- Matthew, Richard, and Mary traveled from Boston, Lincolnshire to **London**, where they stayed with **John Whittingham**
- They sailed to New England in summer 1638, arriving at Boston, Massachusetts approximately late August or early September
- The specific ship has **not been identified**

Matthew was listed as 15 years old in 1638 (supporting b. ~1622/23). He served as barber to Atherton Haugh by 1641.

#### Grandfather's Location — "Burton Cottles, Lincolnshire" (Uncertain)

WikiTree lists the grandfather as being from **"Burton Cottles, Lincolnshire."** This is geographically suspect — "Burton Cottles" is a village in Wiltshire, not Lincolnshire. The likely intended place is **Burton Coggles**, a small village in Kesteven, Lincolnshire (~15 miles south of Grantham, ~20 miles north of Stamford). If accurate, this would place the patrilineal Coy family in the same south Kesteven corridor as the Stamford probate court and the Lenton village hypothesis. This needs verification against the WikiTree source or the Hunt article.

#### Hunt Citation Volume Discrepancy

Two different AI-extracted readings from WikiTree profiles give different volume numbers for the same Hunt article:
